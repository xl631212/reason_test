ACT Scoring Chart: Calculate Your Score

December 13, 2023
3 min read
Contents

Looking to write the ACT but confused about how scoring works? Keep reading, and we’ll tell you everything you need to know about ACT scoring charts!

A comprehensive discussion about college applications includes a few things: campus life, GPA, and standardized tests. Most students are familiar with the SAT, but what about the ACT?

It’s no surprise that being able to show your competence can open doors for you, and postsecondary education is no exception. Scoring well on a standardized test like the SAT and ACT can act as a saving grace for many students that may not have had the resources to perform well on other metrics like GPA.

Before you worry about how to study for the ACT, it’s a good idea to learn how it works. Many students find the ACT score chart somewhat confusing, but it’s actually pretty straightforward once you understand it. 

Without further delay, let's get into the ins and outs of ACT scores!

What Is The ACT?

The ACT is one of two standardized tests that colleges use to assess students’ potential to succeed in a postsecondary environment. Typically, the test is taken towards the end of junior year or the beginning of senior year, with some students opting to take the test more than once.

Understanding The ACT

The key to understanding the ACT is by being familiar with how the test is structured. The test lasts approximately three hours and consists of four sections with an additional writing test, which is optional. The four required sections are English, math, reading, and science.

How The ACT Is Scored

ACT scoring can seem a bit complex at first glance. The process of how your answers are converted to your final score is a multi-step process. Below, we’ll go over things like score methodology, raw vs scale scores, and superscores. 

Scoring Methodology

Probably the first thing you’ll notice about the ACT is that there are far more questions to answer than your final score would suggest. Although your final score is only out of 36, each of the four sections has 40 or more questions. 

The English sections contain 75 questions, and the math section contains 60 questions. Both the reading and science sections have 40 questions each. What’s more, there are categories within each of the sections to give a fair balance of the kinds of questions you’ll encounter.

Raw Scores Vs. Scale Scores

Students often find converting raw scores into scale scores confusing, but what exactly does this mean? 

Well, let’s say you answer 52 out of 75 questions correctly on the English portion of the test. This is your raw English score. Using a conversion chart, this is converted to a score out of 36, which is referred to as your scaled English score. 

You’ll find that the chart doesn’t represent a one-to-one correspondence of your raw score to scale score. That is to say, your raw score isn’t converted into a percentage but simply applied to a 36 total. What this means is that you can actually get a perfect 36 score without actually answering everything correctly.

Average ACT Score

The ACT collects considerable data on how students are performing on their tests every year. The national ranks allow you to compare your score to fresh high school grads who have taken the ACT.

If we take a look at recent figures, the mean scores were as follows: 

  • English: 19.0
  • Math: 19.4
  • Reading: 20.5
  • Science: 20.0
  • Composite score: 19.9

What’s more, the standard deviation is also provided, with composite scores ranging by around six points. This means that about seven out of ten students score within a range of six points above or below the mean, which is about 20. So, scoring between 14 to 26 would put you in line with your peers

When Will You Get Your ACT Score?

With all this talk of ACT scoring methodology, you’re probably wondering when you actually get to see your scores. Looking at the ACT’s score reporting dates will tell you when you can expect to see your results. As a general rule of thumb, expect to wait around two weeks, but be prepared for a wait as long as eight weeks.

Superscoring Explained

Now that you understand the basics of how ACT scores work, you’re probably wondering about how many times you can write the ACT. Let’s say you do very well in the science section from a previous attempt. On another, you outdo yourself in the reading section. Which attempt do colleges see?

Well, fortunately, the ACT has implemented a new scoring methodology called superscoring. Now, you can combine the best scores from all previous attempts, so you don’t have to worry if you tanked a certain subject despite an otherwise stellar performance. What’s more, ACT research shows that superscores are better at predicting college success.

Keep in mind that not all colleges accept superscores. Although the number is increasing, your target school may only accept traditional scoring methods such as your average or highest total score.

Improving Your ACT Score

After writing the ACT, you may find your score lower than that on previous practice tests. In this case, ACT tips and strategies are worth thinking about. Although there isn’t a single best-proven method, having a firm study plan can get you on track to where you need to be.

The ACT actually does a pretty thorough job of breaking down the ACT score chart to show exactly where you performed well and where you missed the mark. It’s advisable to study this feedback and work on areas that need improvement opposed to going over all of the material again.

Practical Tips To Increase Your ACT Score

Asking yourself questions when planning your study sessions can be an effective way to gauge your preparedness for the ACT. For instance, say for the math section, you actually performed fairly well, and it was only geometry that tripped you up. Mastering concepts in this focused manner is better than going over everything.

Try measuring your preparedness with mock tests; it’s been proven that the context in which we learn something plays a crucial role in how well we remember and apply that knowledge. So, try pretending it’s a real test without interruptions to see where you stand.

FAQs

Still have questions about ACT test score charts? Read our facts for more details!

1. Is the ACT Hard?

The ACT in itself isn’t necessarily a hard test. That being said, coming unprepared to any standardized evaluation is bound to be cumbersome. The key to having an easy time with the ACT is making sure you’ve studied the material beforehand. That way, you’re not caught off guard by any surprises.

2. How Is the ACT Writing Section Scored, and Is It Required By All Colleges?

The ACT Writing section is scored separately from the other sections, and it is optional. It's scored on a scale of 2 to 12, with scores based on the quality of your essay and your ability to address the given prompt. 

Not all colleges require the ACT Writing section, so it's important to check the admission requirements of the colleges you're interested in to determine if it's necessary for your application.

3. Can I Take the Act Multiple Times, and Do Colleges Consider My Best Scores?

Yes, you can take the ACT multiple times. Most colleges and universities consider your best scores when evaluating your application. They often use a process called "score choice," allowing you to select which set of scores to send. It's essential to check the policies of the specific colleges you're applying to regarding score reporting.

Final Thoughts

The ACT scoring chart can be a bit counterintuitive if you’re not familiar with it. However, the actual scoring methodology not only gives students opportunities to perform to the best of their abilities, but also to receive targeted feedback in areas which they may be lacking.

Having a deeper understanding of the way standardized tests are scored can lift some anxieties you may have about writing them. When we’re faced with unknowns, there’s a tendency to feel anxious about how things will turn out. The good news is that the ACT is actually a fairly forgiving test, especially with their new superscoring. 

If you ever need help planning or understanding things during your academic journey, speaking to the right people can get you on track to performing your best. This way, you can spend time on things that matter to you, like career planning and professional success. At any rate, the ACT can prove to be a useful tool to show colleges what you’re capable of when it comes time for admission.
